Why this is the best config we found

  • The bottleneck was undertraining, not capacity. The baseline's loss was still dropping at step 2000. A warmup→cosine schedule at a higher peak LR let it actually converge within the step cap — a free −0.18 bpb.
  • The tokenizer multiplies information per step. At ~2.3 bytes/token the BPE packs ~2.3× more context into the same 128-token window and exposes ~2.3× more effective bytes per step — decisive when steps are capped, and biggest on the Hindi (Devanagari cost 3 tokens/char under raw bytes). Another −0.19 bpb.
  • Textbook GPT-2 tricks backfired. Weight tying, init_std 0.02, residual scaling and weight decay all raised bpb (R3/R4). In a 1.5M-param, 2000-step regime they shrink the early signal and slow optimization instead of regularizing an overfit that isn't happening. Knowing why they lost is why we reverted them.
  • The tokenizer stays lossless (all 256 byte values are base tokens with a byte fallback), so per-byte scoring remains valid and the model never fails on unseen UTF-8.

Machine-done vs human-done

The coding agent (Claude) wrote the BPE tokenizer, the tunable trainer (schedule, AdamW, clipping, config plumbing), executed and scored every run, and drafted the RUNLOG / NOTES / this page. The human set the strategy and priorities (attack schedule and tokenizer first, treat data-per-step as the real constraint), made the calls to bundle then isolate the init/regularization experiments, decided to stop the higher-LR run early, chose the final checkpoint, and reviewed every conclusion for correctness. Roughly: implementation and execution ≈ machine; direction, judgement, and stopping decisions ≈ human.

Reproduce: python tokenizer.py --data ../data/train_corpus.txt --vocab 1024 then python train.py --data ../data/train_corpus.txt --steps 2000 --tokenizer bpe --lr 1.5e-3 --schedule cosine --warmup 100 --out ckpt.pt. Score: python evaluate.py --checkpoint ckpt.pt --text_file ../data/dev_eval.txt.
